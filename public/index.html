Problem statement

Many data standards have emerged over recent decades developed by dedicated communities to formalise how data are described, exchanged, published and used. They provide common frameworks and languages reducing ambiguity, improving quality and dramatically improving the exchange efficiency of digital information.

The need for interoperability between these standards is increasing rapidly driven by the opportunities to utilise data from other domains, and the valuable insights that can be derived by combining data from diverse sources.

However, it's hard for practitioners to make these standards interoperable without a clear understanding of the whole landscape, what each standard does and how it can work with others. Most standards are designed for specific use cases. Each has its own terminology. Across the standards, different names are used for the same concept and sometimes a term like 'category' has different meanings.

Four things are needed to advance interoperability between data standards:

  • An agreed definition of what interoperability means
  • A comprehensive catalogue of interoperability use cases
  • A dictionary translating terminology between standards
  • A feature comparison matrix providing a single overview of all relevant standards
Interoperability definition

So, what do we mean by interoperability between data standards?

In the context of government, finance, and policy, data standards interoperability refers to the seamless sharing, integration, and use of data across diverse systems, agencies, and jurisdictions. By enabling compatibility between different data standards—such as SDMX for statistical data, XBRL for financial reporting, and Linked Open Data for semantic linking—interoperability ensures that data from multiple sources can be efficiently combined, compared, and analyzed. This capacity is essential for informed decision-making and supports diverse use cases, from policy analysis to public transparency.

The following forms of interoperability are covered by datastandardsinteroperability.org
  • Technical Interoperability
    • Definition: Ensures that IT systems and infrastructure, including hardware and software, can communicate and share data effectively.
    • Focus: Protocols, data formats, network services, APIs, and file structures.
    • Example: Using APIs to enable direct data transfer between systems, such as data-sharing platforms for government agencies.
  • Syntactic Interoperability
    • Definition: Ensures that data can be exchanged between systems with a shared structure or syntax, enabling accurate data parsing and format consistency.
    • Focus: Data structure and encoding, ensuring data is formatted consistently across systems.
    • Example: XML, JSON, and CSV standards that ensure data from one system can be processed by another without formatting errors.
  • Semantic Interoperability
    • Definition: Ensures that the meaning of data is preserved when exchanged across systems, so that data is interpreted consistently by all parties.
    • Focus: Shared vocabularies, ontologies, taxonomies, and metadata standards.
    • Example: Using Linked Open Data or RDF with standardized vocabularies to ensure that different agencies interpret terms like “income” or “emission” consistently across datasets.
  • Complimentary Interoperability
    • Definition: Standards can be used together to solve specific business problems.
    • Focus: Data lifecycle.
    • Example: Using DDI to gather granular unit record data from which statistics can be calculated and managed by SDMX.
Out of scope
  • Organizational Interoperability
    • Definition: Focuses on aligning policies, processes, and procedures across organizations to facilitate collaboration and data-sharing.
    • Focus: Governance frameworks, regulatory agreements, and process alignment.
    • Example: Cross-agency agreements that define data-sharing protocols and privacy measures, ensuring that all stakeholders follow consistent guidelines.
  • Legal Interoperability
    • Definition: Ensures that data-sharing practices comply with legal standards and regulations across different jurisdictions or organizations.
    • Focus: Legal frameworks, data privacy laws, data ownership rights, and compliance requirements.
    • Example: Harmonizing data-sharing practices between EU and non-EU countries by ensuring GDPR compliance across systems.
  • Policy Interoperability
    • Definition: Establishes common policy frameworks that support aligned objectives and consistent data usage across agencies and sectors.
    • Focus: Shared objectives, data governance policies, and standardized guidelines.
    • Example: Adopting shared policies for open data publication and access, which support cross-agency and cross-sector data integration for public use.
Business case for enhancing data standards interoperability

Interoperability allows policymakers to combine labor statistics from national agencies with financial data from central banks, enabling a comprehensive view of economic health. It also facilitates international reporting, where datasets like economic indicators and tax data must align across borders for accurate global assessments. By connecting these datasets through interoperable standards, agencies avoid duplication, streamline compliance, and support a unified approach to data-driven initiatives, such as tracking sustainable development goals (SDGs).

Ultimately, data standards interoperability strengthens data governance, enriches analysis, and fosters accountability by ensuring that diverse data sources speak the same "language." This integrated framework is key to efficient data use, driving evidence-based policies and fostering public trust through open, accessible data across government and society.
